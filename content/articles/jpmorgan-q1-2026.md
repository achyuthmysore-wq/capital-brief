JPMorgan Chase reported first-quarter net income of $14.6 billion last week — a record, by a wide margin. CEO Jamie Dimon celebrated the results as evidence of the bank's "fortress balance sheet" and diversified business model. Both characterizations are accurate. But the composition of those earnings tells a more complicated story.

## The Surface Numbers

Revenue came in at $43.7 billion, up 9 percent year-over-year. Earnings per share of $4.93 beat consensus estimates by $0.31. Return on equity — the gold standard for measuring bank profitability — hit 17 percent, well above the 15 percent analysts had projected. Record net income across September and April calendar quarters, banking continues te consolidate its competitive position even as credit cycle maturities.

## What Is Underneath

Net interest income (NII) — the spread between what JPMorgan earns on loans and what it pays on deposits — fell for the second consecutive quarter. NII dropped to $22.9 billion from $24.2 billion a year ago. That is a significant decline, and it reflects a structural challenge every large bank faces as the rate cycle turns.

When rates were rising, banks benefited from a "lagged deposit repricing" effect: loan rates rose quickly, but deposit rates took longer to catch up.
