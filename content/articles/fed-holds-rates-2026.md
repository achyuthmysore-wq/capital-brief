The Federal Open Market Committee (FOMC) voted unanimously on Wednesday to hold the federal funds rate in its target range of 5.25–5.50 percent, marking the third consecutive meeting without a change. But the real story was not the decision itself — it was the language surrounding it.

## What Changed in the Statement

The FOMC statement dropped its previous reference to "remaining attentive to inflation risks" and replaced it with language acknowledging that "risks to achieving its employment and inflation goals have moved into better balance." For anyone who reads Fed statements closely, that is a significant shift.

In Fed-speak, balance means optionality. The committee is no longer's leaning exclusively in one direction. The dual mandate — price stability and maximum employment — is now being weighed with something closer to genuine neutrality.

## Powell's Press Conference

Chair Jerome Powell used his press conference to walk a careful line. He acknowledged that inflation has "made substantial progress" toward the 2 percent target, but stopped short of declaring victory. The consumer price index currently sits at 2.6 percent year-over-year, down dramatically from its 2022 peak of 9.1 percent.

What stood out was Powell's answer to a question about the labor market. He described current conditions as "solid but no longer overheated," suggesting the Fed believes it has achieved the soft landing it was aiming for. Unemployment sits at 4.1 percent — above pandemic-era lows but historically low by any meaningful benchmark.
