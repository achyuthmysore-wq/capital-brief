For the third consecutive Federal Open Market Committee (FOMC) meeting, the Federal Reserve voted unanimously to hold its benchmark federal funds rate steady in the 5.25–5.50% range. On the surface, this is unremarkable. But the real story is in what Chair Jerome Powell said afterward.

## The Language Shift That Markets Noticed

In previous post-meeting press conferences, Powell emphasized patience and data dependence — code for "we're not moving anytime soon." Wednesday's language was different. For the first time since the hiking cycle began in March 2022, Powell explicitly acknowledged that the *next* move is more likely to be a cut than a hike.

That might sound obvious. But for a central bank that has spent two years fighting inflation, it marks a genuine turning point in the communication strategy.

> "We believe policy is well positioned. The question now is timing, not direction."
> — Chair Jerome Powell, April 2026

## Why the Fed Is Cautious

Three data points are keeping the Fed in hold mode longer than markets had hoped:

**Core PCE remains sticky.** The Fed's preferred inflation measure — Personal Consumption Expenditures excluding food and energy — came in at 2.8% year-over-year in February. That's progress from the 5.4% peak in early 2022, but it's still nearly a full percentage point above the Fed's 2% target.

**The labor market refuses to crack.** Unemployment sits at 3.9%, and monthly job creation continues to surprise to the upside. A hot labor market means wage growth, and wage growth means services inflation stays elevated. The Fed can't declare victory on inflation while workers are still getting 4–5% annual raises.

**Financial conditions have loosened prematurely.** Every time the Fed signals potential cuts, the stock market rallies and mortgage rates drop — which stimulates the economy and risks reigniting inflation. It's a feedback loop the Fed is keenly aware of.

## What History Tells Us About This Moment

The 1994–1995 hiking cycle offers an instructive parallel. The Fed raised rates aggressively, then paused for seven months before cutting. That soft landing — cooling inflation without triggering a recession — is the model Powell has explicitly cited.

The key difference today: in 1995, inflation was already back near target when the cuts began. In 2026, the Fed would be cutting with inflation still slightly elevated. That's a riskier bet, and it explains the caution.

## What This Means for Markets

Bond markets are pricing in two cuts by year-end, with the first arriving in September. Equity markets have largely priced in the same scenario. If inflation data continues to cool, that timeline holds. If we get a surprise spike — say, from energy prices or senewed supply chain disruptions — the first cut gets pushed to 2027.

For investors, the current environment favors:

- **Investment-grade bonds** that benefit from eventual rate normalization
- **Rate-sensitive sectors** like real estate and utilities, which have underperformed during the hiking cycle
- **Cash caution** — money market funds at 5%+ yields won't last, making the opportunity cost of staying in cash increasingly real

## The Bottom Line

The Fed is not cutting yet. But the debate has shifted from "will they cut?" to "when?" That's a meaningful change in posture, and markets are right to pay attention to it. The question for the next three meetings is whether the inflation data cooperates — or forces another delay.
